# Baseline Knowledge Diagnostic Card

## Purpose

This card is distributed at the start of Saturday Morning Block 1 (10:28–10:34). Students complete it individually in silence in three minutes. It is not graded and there are no wrong answers.

The instructor uses the results to calibrate cold calls, identify where to spend extra teaching time, and build an initial picture of cohort readiness across the two days.

**Instruction to students:** Rate your familiarity with each concept honestly. This is for the instructor's use only.

## INSTRUCTOR SCORING GUIDE

*(Do not distribute to students)*

### How to use the results

**After collecting cards (during break 10:50–11:00):**

Sort cards into three groups:

- **High readiness** (mostly 4–5): These students are candidates for nuanced extensions, devil's advocate positions, and leading case discussions. Call on them when you want a sophisticated argument challenged.

- **Mid readiness** (mostly 2–3): This is the target audience for framework teaching. Use them as primary voices for recall questions and framework explanation.

- **Low readiness** (mostly 1–2): Call on these students early and often with well-bounded questions to build confidence. Avoid ambushing them with open-ended conceptual questions in the first block.

### Calibration rules

- Call on 1–2 rated students more frequently in early sessions. Ask bounded questions: "Can you name one of Rogers' five domains?" not "Explain Rogers' framework."

- Call on 4–5 rated students for nuanced extensions: "You said Uber is disruptive. What would Christensen say about that and why might he be wrong?"

- Never let 4–5 rated students dominate the room. Redirect: "That's one view — [student with low rating], what do you think of that argument?"

### Cross-reference with team assignments

Note the distribution of high, mid, and low readiness within each team. If one team has all the high-readiness students, redistribute cold-call attention to balance the classroom energy.

### Common patterns and responses

| Pattern | What It Means | Response |
|---|---|---|
| High Christensen familiarity, low MECE | Strong strategy background, weak consulting toolkit | Teach MECE as a discipline, not as theory. Apply it immediately. |
| High Porter, low Christensen | Business school background without the disruption literature | Spend extra time distinguishing disruption from competitive strategy |
| High AI familiarity, low ROI modelling | Technology background without finance skills | Pair with finance-strong students in team structure |
| Low across the board | No prior strategy exposure | Move slower through framework teaching; use more examples |
| High across the board | Strong cohort | Challenge more; push to application faster; reduce teaching time, increase case time |
